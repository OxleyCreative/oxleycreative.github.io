Making a Complicated Purchase Easier to Understand

Tire shopping combines unfamiliar terminology, subtle performance differences, safety considerations, and a purchase most people make only once every few years. In conversations with consumers, the experience was often described as confusing, overwhelming, expensive, and even scary.

I helped design and build two connected tools to reduce that uncertainty. The Tire Decision Guide helps shoppers translate their needs into relevant recommendations, while the Compare Bar gives more self-directed customers a way to evaluate products they select themselves.

Both tools were designed around the same goal: present enough information to support a confident decision without asking customers to become tire experts first.

Audience
Customers purchasing tires, wheels, and installation services
My role
Product design, UI/UX, front-end development
Domain
Automotive e-commerce, checkout, and fulfillment
6 min read
Turn an Unfamiliar Category Into Understandable Questions

Customers rarely begin by thinking in terms of product specifications. They think about their vehicle, the conditions they encounter, the way they drive, what they want to improve, and how much they are comfortable spending.

The Tire Decision Guide starts with that more familiar language. It gathers vehicle information and driving conditions before asking customers to rate priorities such as price, wet performance, dry performance, winter capability, comfort, and tread life.

Those answers are used to generate up to three recommendations. Instead of presenting the entire catalog and asking customers to interpret every technical difference, the guide narrows the decision to a smaller set of products aligned with the needs they expressed.

Explain the Recommendation, Not Just the Result

A recommendation is only useful if the customer understands why it was made. Without that explanation, a guided tool can feel like a black box asking users to trust an unexplained result.

We created a recommendation summary at the top of the results page that connects the suggested products back to the customer’s answers. It identifies the category and priorities that influenced the result, making the logic of the recommendation more visible.

Customers can revise their priorities and see the recommendations respond. That feedback helps demonstrate that their choices meaningfully affect the result rather than disappearing into a generic questionnaire.

We also provided a way to sign in and save results. Tire purchases are not always completed in a single session, so customers needed to be able to return to their recommendations after researching, discussing options, or simply taking more time to decide.

Build a Shared Language for Comparison

Once customers had a focused set of products, the next challenge was helping them understand the differences. Product pages contained the necessary information, but moving between separate pages placed too much responsibility on memory.

A comparison table gave each product a consistent position and aligned equivalent information across columns. Customers could scan prices, specifications, performance results, reviews, and warranty details without repeatedly navigating away or trying to remember what they had just seen.

The table also created an information-architecture challenge. Tires can have a large number of meaningful attributes, but giving every data point equal visual weight would reproduce the same sense of overload we were trying to reduce. The content needed to be grouped and ordered around the questions customers were most likely to ask.

Keep Customers Oriented in a Long Comparison

Comparison tables are effective because they align information, but they become difficult to use when product names scroll out of view. Customers could easily forget which column belonged to which tire as they moved deeper into the page.

We introduced persistent product headers that remain visible while the customer reviews the table. The headers preserve each product’s identity and keep the purchase action accessible when the customer reaches a decision.

This interaction had to work across screen sizes without obscuring the information below it. On smaller screens, the design needed to preserve orientation while supporting horizontal movement and keeping controls large enough to use comfortably.

fig 2. persistent headers keep products identifiable as customers move through the comparison

Prioritize the Evidence Customers Trust

Not every product attribute carries the same weight. User testing showed that customer reviews and Tire Rack’s performance testing were among the most influential sources of information in the purchase process.

We placed this evidence near the top of the comparison rather than burying it beneath basic specifications. Summary ratings support quick scanning, while expandable details allow customers to investigate specific performance categories such as wet traction, dry traction, winter capability, comfort, and treadwear.

This layered approach serves two different reading behaviors. Customers looking for a quick signal can use the summary, while those who want to understand the tradeoffs can reveal more detail without leaving the comparison.

fig 3. expandable ratings provide detail without overwhelming the initial comparison

Support Shoppers Who Already Have Products in Mind

Not every customer needs a guided recommendation. Some arrive with previous experience, a preferred brand, or a list of products they want to investigate. They still benefit from the comparison experience, but they need control over which products enter it.

We extended the comparison system into search results through the Compare Bar. Customers can select products while browsing and collect them in a persistent drawer without interrupting their search.

The collapsed drawer preserves context and provides feedback about what has been selected. Expanding it gives customers a quick review of the products, prices, and staggered fitments they have collected before committing to the full comparison table.

Reusing the comparison table established for the Decision Guide also meant customers encountered the same patterns, information order, and interaction model whether they received recommendations or selected products independently.

fig 5. the expanded drawer provides a review before entering the full comparison

Design Two Paths Toward the Same Decision

The Tire Decision Guide and Compare Bar serve different starting points, but they are not separate experiences. One helps customers discover what may fit their needs; the other helps them evaluate products they have already found.

Both paths converge on a shared comparison system. That consistency reduces relearning and allows improvements to the table, ratings, headers, and responsive behavior to benefit guided and self-directed shoppers alike.

Thinking about the tools as parts of one decision-support system also helped prevent each feature from becoming an isolated solution. Recommendations, search, comparison, product education, and purchase actions needed to work together as stages of the same journey.

Measure the Result—and Question Surprising Data

Converting the Decision Guide results into the tabular comparison format produced a substantial increase in conversion for customers using that path. During the first few weeks, the result appeared to be roughly two to three times higher than the control.

The initial difference was large enough that we were cautious about attributing all of it to the redesign. Rather than treating an unusually strong early result as a final answer, we continued monitoring the experience as more data became available.

The improvement remained positive over time, supporting the broader design direction: customers were more likely to move forward when recommendations were explainable and meaningful differences could be evaluated in one place.

The work reinforced an important lesson for me. Good decision support is not about removing information. It is about organizing information around the customer’s questions, revealing detail at the right moment, and making tradeoffs easier to understand.
