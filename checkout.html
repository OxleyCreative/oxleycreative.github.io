Re-Imagining the Checkout Experience

Checkout is where every decision made during the shopping experience must come together. For Tire Rack customers, that means more than entering an address and payment method. The experience may need to support home delivery, shipment to an installer, appointment scheduling, local pickup, and product-specific installation requirements.

I helped redesign and build the checkout around those different journeys. Our goal was to preserve the flexibility customers needed while making each path feel clear, predictable, and intentionally designed.

Audience
Customers purchasing tires, wheels, and installation services
My role
Product design, UI/UX, front-end development
Domain
Automotive e-commerce, checkout, and fulfillment
6 min read
Begin With the System, Not the Screens

Before redesigning individual pages, we mapped the existing checkout from beginning to end. This gave us a shared view of the decisions, dependencies, and alternate paths that had accumulated across the experience.

The exercise helped us distinguish necessary complexity from complexity created by the interface itself. Some questions were essential to fulfillment and installation, but they were not always being asked at the most useful moment. By restructuring the flow first, we could simplify the experience without removing information the business or customer still needed.

Follow the Friction

Reviewing the flow revealed how front-loaded checkout had become. Depending on their selections, customers could encounter multiple dialogs asking for information or decisions before they were able to leave the cart.

Customers who completed those steps were then presented with another decision: sign in or continue as a guest. Analytics showed significant abandonment at this point, making the account screen an important place to investigate more closely. Rather than treating the drop-off as an isolated page problem, we considered the friction created by everything customers had already encountered before reaching it.

fig 3. behavioral data helped identify a significant point of abandonment

Test the Assumptions at the Account Gate

User testing showed that many customers did not expect to encounter a dedicated login screen at this stage. Some did not notice the guest-checkout option, while others were unsure whether they had previously created an account.

That uncertainty was especially important in this category. Most people purchase tires only occasionally, so remembering a password—or even remembering that an account exists—cannot be treated as a dependable part of the journey.

We explored a more balanced account screen that made guest checkout easier to recognize and reduced the visual dominance of signing in. We also considered faster authentication options that could help returning customers avoid password-related frustration.

fig 4. a revised account screen gave guest checkout greater visibility

Explore a Guest-First Alternative

We also explored removing the account gate entirely and allowing customers to enter checkout as guests by default. This direction eliminated an interruption and let customers continue while their purchase intent was strongest.

The tradeoff was that returning customers might proceed without using saved account information. To preserve that value without blocking progress, we designed a persistent but unobtrusive login reminder. Customers could sign in when it became useful, rather than being required to make that decision before moving forward.

Comparing these directions helped us evaluate more than the appearance of a login page. It reframed the larger question: should account access function as a gate, or as a helpful capability available throughout checkout?

fig 5. the login reminder kept account access available without blocking the guest path

Make Progress Visible and Decisions Reversible

Customers also lacked a clear mental model of the checkout. They did not know how long the process would be, what information they would need, or when fulfillment and installation decisions would appear.

We developed an interactive step header to address three related needs. First, it showed customers where they were and provided a sense of how much remained. Second, it created a place to review information gathered across the journey. Third, it allowed customers to return to an earlier decision without losing their orientation.

The expanded summary made the checkout feel less like a sequence of disconnected forms and more like one manageable process. It also supported error recovery by making previous choices visible and editable.

fig 6. the step header communicated progress throughout checkout

fig 7. the expanded view allowed customers to review and revisit earlier information

Ask for Information in Context

The original cart experience could trigger several dialogs before customers understood how each question related to their purchase. Although the information was necessary, its timing made it feel like an obstacle.

We removed those information-gathering dialogs from the transition out of the cart and reorganized their questions as substeps within the main checkout. This preserved the required business logic while giving each decision a clearer context.

Progressive disclosure was especially useful for installation. Appointment scheduling, installation addresses, tire positioning, and product-specific acknowledgements could appear when relevant without forcing every customer through the same amount of detail.

fig 8. expandable substeps presented installation details when they became relevant

Create One Structure for Many Journeys

The central design challenge was not creating one perfect linear checkout. It was creating a dependable structure that could support several valid journeys without making customers learn a different interface for each one.

Shared patterns for progress, summaries, expandable substeps, validation, and editing created a consistent foundation. Within that foundation, the experience could adapt to home delivery, installer fulfillment, appointment scheduling, or local pickup while still feeling like the same product.

Designing responsive prototypes also forced us to consider how these decisions behaved with less space. Complex scheduling controls, summaries, and installation requirements needed to remain understandable on mobile without simply compressing the desktop layout.

Prototype, Build, and Keep Iterating

This was a front-to-back reconsideration of the checkout rather than a visual refresh. The work moved between journey maps, interface concepts, prototypes, user feedback, front-end implementation, and continued refinement.

The resulting design established a clearer foundation for checkout: reduce unnecessary gates, introduce decisions in context, communicate progress, and make previous choices easy to review. The live experience continues to evolve, but those principles remain useful whenever new fulfillment options, installation services, or business requirements are introduced.
